overview
Snack Attack was a project completed for Interaction Design Studio I at Carnegie Mellon University. The goal of our project was to utilize responsive design to address a decrease in customer browsing at grocery stores due to the pandemic. In particular, our solution was tailored for Whole Foods as our fictional client.

user research
Our project began with directed storytelling interviews to gain an understanding of the current grocery shopping experience. After creating a consolidated customer journey map for fifteen participants, we identified the following insights that informed our ideation and design.

01. Customers were typically prepared with mental or physical lists prior to entering the grocery store.
02. Due to the pandemic, customers preferred to spend less time in-store; grocery shopping became a rushed process.
03. Customers felt guilty when impulsively purchasing snacks or new products at the grocery store.


At this point, we decided to focus our attention on improving the browsing experience for customers while increasing impulse buying. To identify the value in this market space, we created a value diagram for shoppers and Whole Foods -- or our users and our client, respectively.

ideation and prototyping
Next, our team ideated around several ideas that incorporated the user insights uncovered during interviews. We ultimately landed on a browsing opportunity centered around event-planning: a Whole Foods extension for Google Calendar that provided snack recommendations for various events. Users would have the opportunity to browse, purchase snacks, or add snacks to event description directly in their calendar.



By connecting snack-browsing directly to an event, the extension would reduce guilt by assigning a clear purpose to each snack purchase.
